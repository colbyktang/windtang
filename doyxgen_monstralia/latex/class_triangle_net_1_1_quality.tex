A subsegment is encroached if there is a vertex in its diametral lens. For Ruppert\textquotesingle{}s algorithm (-\/D switch), the \char`\"{}diametral lens\char`\"{} is the diametral circle. For Chew\textquotesingle{}s algorithm (default), the diametral lens is just big enough to enclose two isosceles triangles whose bases are the subsegment. Each of the two isosceles triangles has two angles equal to \textquotesingle{}b.\+minangle\textquotesingle{}.

Chew\textquotesingle{}s algorithm does not require diametral lenses at all--but they save time. Any vertex inside a subsegment\textquotesingle{}s diametral lens implies that the triangle adjoining the subsegment will be too skinny, so it\textquotesingle{}s only a matter of time before the encroaching vertex is deleted by Chew\textquotesingle{}s algorithm. It\textquotesingle{}s faster to simply not insert the doomed vertex in the first place, which is why I use diametral lenses with Chew\textquotesingle{}s algorithm. \mbox{\Hypertarget{class_triangle_net_1_1_quality_a1081a82553e7481bb34c86b9a02d6fd3}\label{class_triangle_net_1_1_quality_a1081a82553e7481bb34c86b9a02d6fd3}}
